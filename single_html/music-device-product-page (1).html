WHY WOULD YOU BUY WHAT YOU CAN BUILD YOURSELF?

Welcome to the anti-commercial, pro-DIY noise machine manifesto. This isn't a store - it's a resource for anyone who wants to create their own experimental sound devices without feeding the capitalist machine.

"The most beautiful thing we can experience is the mysterious. It is the source of all true art and science."
— Albert Einstein (but who cares about authority figures anyway)

If you're looking for slick, polished products with warranties and customer service, you're in the wrong place. We believe in:

  • Building instead of buying
  • Community knowledge over corporate secrets
  • Noise as liberation from musical constraints
  • Circuit-bending as a revolutionary act
  • The beauty of chaos and imperfection

CIRCUIT DIAGRAMS & BUILDS

Here are some basic noise machines you can build with easily salvaged parts. Most components can be found in old electronics or bought cheaply. Don't pay for expensive boutique synths when you can make your own.

ATARI PUNK CONSOLE

The classic noise-maker. Two 555 timer ICs creating square wave tones that'll make your neighbors question your sanity.

Difficulty: Beginner

FEEDBACK DRONE MACHINE

Create endless walls of sound using simple audio feedback loops and distortion circuits. Perfect for deep meditation or annoying your roommates.

Difficulty: Intermediate

CONTACT MIC CHAOS

Turn ordinary objects into instruments by amplifying their microscopic vibrations. Then destroy the signal with various circuit-bent effects.

Difficulty: Beginner

DIGITAL GLITCH SYNTH

Use microcontrollers to create chaotic, unstable digital noise. Embrace the errors, clicks, and pops of digital processing gone wrong.

Difficulty: Advanced

PARTS LIST

  • 2 × 555 Timer ICs Salvage from old electronics or dumpster diving
  • R1: 10kΩ Resistor Literally anywhere
  • R3: 1kΩ Resistor Literally anywhere
  • R5: 100kΩ Resistor Literally anywhere
  • C1: 0.01μF Capacitor Old circuit boards
  • C2: 0.1μF Capacitor Old circuit boards
  • R4: 100kΩ Potentiometer Old radios, TVs, etc.
  • 9V Battery Clip Dead smoke detectors
  • Audio Jack (1/8" or 1/4") Broken headphones

BUILD NOTES

This circuit produces a square wave sound that can be modified by adjusting the potentiometer. It's the basis of countless punk noise boxes since the 1980s.

Experiment with different capacitor values to change the sound range. Smaller capacitors = higher frequencies.
